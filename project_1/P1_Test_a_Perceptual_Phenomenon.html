Background information¶

In a Stroop task, participants are presented with a list of words, with each word displayed in a color of ink. The participants task is to say out loud the color of the ink in which the word is printed. The task has two conditions: a congruent words condition, and an incongruent words condition. In the congruent words condition, the words being displayed are color words whose names match the colors in which they are printed: for example RED, BLUE. In the incongruent words condition, the words displayed are color words whose names do not match the colors in which they are printed: for example PURPLE, ORANGE. In each case, we measure the time it takes to name the ink colors in equally-sized lists. Each participant will go through and record a time from each condition.

Q1: What is our independent variable? What is our dependent variable?¶

The independent variable is whether the word name and color are the same or different. Level of measurement is word-color match, or word-color mismatch. Time it takes going through a list of words is dependent on word-color matching or not. (Ref. /1/)

Q2: What is an appropriate set of hypotheses for this task? What kind of statistical test do you expect to perform? Justify your choices.¶

In this test, we have assessed human ability to process letters and colors. A sample of $n = 24$ persons have performed the Stroop experiment. They went through two different word lists, containing color names (red, blue, green and etc.). In the first list, the word and color of the ink were same. The persons had to go through the list and say the name which is described by the letters. This is called the congruent case. The other list had a different ink color, than the letters spell. The volunteers needed to say the color of the ink, so-called incongruent case. A dataset containing the time each person needed to go through both lists was given to us in this experiment.

A hypothesis test will be performed to assess whether we have enough evidence from the sample, $X$, to infer evidence for the entire population, $U$ ref. /4/. Is there a real difference in the population, or are the sample result picked by chance? From the dataset, we will compare the sample mean times, namely sample mean congruent time ($\bar{X}_C$), and sample mean incongruent time ($\bar{X}_I$). They will be assessed in the hypothesis test, to infer evidence for the difference between the real population means, $\mu_C$ and $\mu_I$.

Proposed hypothesis test:¶

  • $H_0: \mu_C - \mu_I = 0$
  • $H_a: \mu_C - \mu_I < 0 $
  • $H_0$: Null hypothesis. In our case, it states that the population parameters $\mu_C$ and $\mu_I$ are the same.
  • $H_a$: Alternative hypothesis. It states that the population parameters differ. In our case, we assume that $\mu_C$ is smaller than $\mu_I$. Meaning that people tend to process semantics faster than colors.

Choosing to perform a t-test¶

We are going to perform a t-test based on these criteria (ref. /4/ and ref. /5/):

  1. Assessing the sample means, $\bar{X}_I$ and $\bar{X}_C$.
  2. We do not know the population standard deviation, $\sigma$.
  3. Sample is less than 30 ($n=24$).
  4. Assuming a normally distributed population.

Also, since same person goes through both the congruent and incongruent case, this test is an dependent t-test.

This hypothesis test will be performed using sample mean statistics:¶

As we do not have information about the population parameters and we are performing a t-test from a sample, we will apply the sample mean statistics in our hypothesis test:

  • $H_0: \bar{X}_C - \bar{X}_I = 0$
  • $H_a: \bar{X}_C- \bar{X}_I < 0 $

Testing the hypothesis¶

We will now perform the hypothesis test, which was assigned in Q2 above. With the stated alternative hypothesis, $H_a$, we will perform a left-tailed test. To calculate the t-statistic, we will use equation (ref. /5/):

$$t = \frac{\bar{X_C}-\bar{X_I}}{\frac{s_D}{\sqrt{n}}}$$
  • $\bar{X}_I$: Average time of the incongruent sample.
  • $\bar{X}_C$: Average time of the congruent sample.
  • $s_D$ : Standard deviation for the congruent and incongruent samples difference
  • $n$ : Number of people in the sample.

An $\alpha$-value of .05 is chosen for this purpose and will be used to compute the critical t-value. A sample result is called statistically significant when the p-value for a test statistic is less than or equal to the level of significance. This is also known as the $\alpha$-level. A p-value may be seen as the following conditional probability: $P(\textrm{Reject } H_0 \textrm{ | } H_0 \textrm{ is true})$. It is probability of performing a Type I error, ref. /6/.

In [8]:
# Critical t-stat
alpha = 0.05
n = df_c.count()
dof = n - 1
t_crit = -stats.t.ppf(1 - alpha, dof)
print "Critical t-stat: %g" % t_crit

# Standard error
SE = s_d / np.sqrt(n)
# Calculate the sample t-stat
t_stat = (xbar_c - xbar_i) / SE
print "Sample t-stat: %g" % t_stat

Critical t-stat: -1.71387
Sample t-stat: -8.02071

We see that the calculated t-stat significantly smaller than t-crit, which means that we can reject null hypo. with a $p \leq .05$.

Confidece interval¶

A 95% confidence interval between the mean difference will be constructed. Since the incongruent average time is greatest, the difference evaluated will be: $\bar{X}_D = \bar{X}_I - \bar{X}_C$. We used a directional t-test previously, so we need to calculate a new, two-tailed t-crit for the confidence interval.

For the calculation of confidence interval, this equation will be applied:

$$\bar{X}_D \pm t \cdot \frac{s_D}{\sqrt{n}}$$

The $t$-value is computed using the 95% confidence interval in the middle of the distribution. Thus, the $\alpha$-level used to calculate the $t$-value have to be divided in half. The probability in both tails is .025.

In [9]:
# The difference 
xbar_d = (df_i - df_c).mean()
# 95% confidence interval
alpha = 0.05
# Two tailed now
t = stats.t.ppf(1 - alpha/2, dof)
print "t-value: %g" % t_crit

# Calculate the confidence interval for the mean difference
conf_int = [(xbar_d + (t*SE) * n) for n in (-1, 1)]

print "xbar_d: %g" % xbar_d
print "CI: [%s]" % ", ".join([str(ci) for ci in conf_int])

Conclusion¶

A statistical t-test was performed demonstrating human ability to process letters and colors. Each person in the sample evaluated equally long lists of words with word-color match (congruent case), and non word-color match (incongruent case). The mean time going through the lists was used as input for the hypothesis. As the same person performed both tests, the time going through both lists is dependent. Thus, dependent t-test was performed.

Our null hypothesis assumed no difference in the mean time. Our alternative hypothesis assumed that the mean time to go through the incongruent list is greater.

The statistical test was performed using a one-tailed t-test and an $\alpha$-level of .05. The null hypothesis was rejected, showing that it is statistically significant with p-value $\leq$ .05. It takes longer time to process color rather than the semantics.

The dataset results listed mean times of 14.05 s and 22.02 s for the congruent and incongruent lists, respectively. The mean difference is 7.96 s, with a 95% confidence interval of [5.91, 10.02]. Thus, the interval further implies that it takes longer time to process color over letters, as it is above zero.
